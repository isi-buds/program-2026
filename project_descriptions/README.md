# ISI-BUDS 2025 Research Project Descriptions

## Neuropsychiatric Symptoms in Down Syndrome: Assessing Risk for Cognitive Decline in the Alzheimer’s Disease Continuum

### Mentoring Team:  

- David Sultzer, Clinical Core Lead at Alzheimer’s Disease Research Center, UCI  
- Daniel Gillen, Chancellor’s Professor of Statistics, UCI  
- Christy Hom, Associate Clinical Professor of Psychiatry, UCI  
- Sean Leader, Statistics PhD Student, UCI

### Background: 

Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) has become a world-wide healthcare crisis characterized by abnormal amyloid protein plaques in the brain. With the advancing age of populations globally, the prevalence of AD is expected to increase dramatically, leading to enormous medical morbidity, family challenges, and healthcare costs. In typical sporadic cases of AD, memory impairment usually begins after age 70\. However, people with Down Syndrome (DS) are at a very high risk of developing AD early in adulthood due to the overexpression of amyloid precursor protein associated with triplication of chromosome 21\. Most individuals with DS will develop amyloid plaques by age 40 and experience progressive cognitive decline by age 50\.

Neuropsychiatric symptoms (NPS) such as depression, anxiety, irritability, apathy, and agitation are common in typical late-life onset AD (LOAD), contributing to patient morbidity, caregiver stress, and institutionalization. Recent evidence indicates that later-life psychiatric symptoms are a risk factor for AD, and that new-onset mild psychiatric symptoms, even in the absence of memory difficulties, are associated with progression to mild cognitive impairment and AD dementia. Individuals with DS face a similar risk for NPS early in the development of dementia. The pattern of NPS in DS mirrors that of LOAD, suggesting that the shared amyloid neuropathology contributes prominently.

Although NPS become increasingly frequent across cognitive stages (Cognitively Stable, Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI), AD Dementia) among those with DS, it is currently unknown whether these symptoms are associated with a more rapid progression of cognitive decline among cognitively stable individuals. Furthermore, it remains unclear how NPS influence the transition from MCI to AD dementia, or how they relate to the continued worsening of cognitive deficits once AD dementia is established. Clarifying these relationships can confirm if NPS are fundamental expressions of AD pathology that accelerate cognitive decline, or if alternative non-amyloid processes are responsible. Furthermore, understanding the specific NPS in DS can help individuals, families, and clinicians recognize non-memory symptoms of cognitive decline, which improves differential diagnosis, reduces mental health stigma, and reveals opportunities for early targeted treatments.

### Study Design and Data Description:

This longitudinal cohort study will assess NPS and cognitive skills over time in individuals with DS. Data comes from the ABC-DS national cohort (Freeze 6), featuring up to six assessment cycles collected at roughly 16-month intervals. Participants with conditions such as Parkinsonism, traumatic brain injury, and drug or alcohol abuse will be excluded due to key clinical differences from the target population.

The sample consists of 542 individuals measured across 1,216 total observations. At baseline the cohort’s cognitive diagnostic breakdown is as follows: 413 are cognitively stable, 63 have mild cognitive impairment, 52 have dementia, and 14 have a status of “Unable to Determine”. The primary analysis will use Neuropsychiatric Inventory (NPI) total and item scores, incorporating frequency and severity across 12 symptom domains (e.g. depression, irritability, appetite, etc.). To measure cognitive change, the study will analyze shifts in clinical diagnosis, cognition scores (such as the MoCA or DS Mental Status Exam), and measures of verbal memory and executive function. Statistical models will incorporate covariates and potential confounders, including demographics (e.g. age, sex, race, ethnicity, education) and available health data (e.g. sleep apnea, seizures, medication).

## Objectives: 

1. Replicate and validate the baseline *cross-sectional* association between NPS and cognitive diagnosis within the Down Syndrome cohort previously reported in Magaña-Ramirez et. al. (2025).  
2. Quantify whether *longitudinal* changes in cognition are dependent on baseline NPS within the DS cohort. Change in cognition is measured in a few ways:  
   1. Change in diagnosis (to MCI or Dementia) at future visits  
   2. Change in global cognition score on neuropsychological assessments  
   3. Change in composite measure of executive skills and verbal memory  
3. (Optional) Evaluate the relationship between baseline NPS and AD biomarkers, and examine for interaction between baseline NPS and baseline amyloid level in the development of cognitive deficits over time.

**Title**: Environmental Drivers of *Aedes aegypti* Abundance in the Coachella Valley

**Mentoring Team:**

Daniel M. Parker, Associate Professor, Public Health  
Volodymyr Minin; Professor, Statistics  
Thanasi Bakis; PhD candidate, Statistics

**Background**

Mosquito-borne diseases remain one of the world's most important public health challenges. *Aedes aegypti*, the primary mosquito vector of dengue fever, Zika, chikungunya, and yellow fever, has expanded rapidly throughout California since it was first detected in the state in 2013\. Today it has become established in hundreds of cities across Southern and Central California and continues to spread into new environments.

Unlike many mosquito species, *Ae. aegypti* is highly adapted to urban environments. It breeds in small artificial containers, feeds primarily on humans during the daytime, and can persist even in hot, dry climates where natural surface water is limited. Understanding why mosquito populations become abundant in some places and at certain times of year — but not others — is useful and helpful for designing effective mosquito surveillance and control programs.

Environmental conditions strongly influence mosquito biology. Temperature affects development rates, survival, biting frequency, and viral replication (among those infected), while land cover, vegetation, elevation, and urban structure all influence the habitats available for mosquitoes. Many of these environmental variables can now be measured using Earth observation data collected by satellites and other sources, providing opportunities to investigate mosquito ecology across large geographic areas. However, satellite-derived climate products often have relatively coarse spatial resolution and may not accurately represent the fine-scale microclimates that mosquitoes actually experience near the ground.

This project combines mosquito surveillance data, weather station observations, and satellite-derived environmental data to better understand the environmental drivers of *Ae. aegypti* abundance in California's Coachella Valley. Students will work with real ecological and public health datasets while learning methods in spatial analysis, environmental data science, and statistical modeling.

**Study Design and Data Description**

This project will use mosquito surveillance data collected by local vector control agencies in the Coachella Valley between 2016 and 2025\. The dataset contains thousands of mosquito trap observations collected repeatedly across the valley over multiple years. Information available includes trap locations, collection dates, trap type, trapping effort, and numbers of adult Ae. aegypti mosquitoes captured.

These data will be combined with multiple environmental datasets, including:

Satellite-derived land use and land cover (Dynamic World)  
Elevation and topographic variables  
Weather and climate products (e.g., ERA5)  
Weather station observations and mobile temperature logger data collected in the field during summer 2026

Students may work on one or more components of the project, including exploratory data analysis, visualization, spatial analysis, environmental data processing, statistical modeling, and predictive modeling.

**Objectives**

The project will investigate several related questions, including:

What temporal, spatial, or spatio-temporal patterns exist in *Ae. aegypti* abundance across the Coachella Valley?  
Which environmental variables best explain variation in mosquito abundance across space and time?  
How well do commonly used weather and climate products represent the temperatures that mosquitoes actually experience?  
Can fine-scale microclimate estimates improve our ability to predict mosquito abundance?

Depending on student interests and progress, additional analyses may include spatial clustering, spatial synchrony, machine learning approaches, and predictive mapping of mosquito habitat suitability.

**Title**: Sleep-dependent Cognitive Performance in Youth with Obstructive Sleep Apnea before Adenotonsillectomy Surgery

**Mentoring Team**:    
Katharine Simon, Assistant Professor, Department of Pediatrics  
Ana Kenney, Assistant Professor, Department of Statistics  
Asha Pantula, PhD Student, Department of Statistics

**Background:**  
Obstructive sleep apnea syndrome (OSAS), defined by the American Academy of Pediatrics as disordered breathing during sleep that disrupts ventilation and sleep architecture, affects up to \~9.5% of children and is associated with significant neurobehavioral sequelae, including attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder symptoms, cognitive impairments, and deficits in memory and verbal abilities. Although adenotonsillectomy (AT) is the first-line treatment for pediatric OSA, evidence for consistent cognitive and behavioral improvement following surgery is mixed. Prior work has largely focused on changes in sleep architecture (e.g., arousals, efficiency), with limited and inconsistent findings, and has rarely examined how treatment-related changes in sleeping brain rhythms—particularly NREM spectral power—relate to neurobehavioral outcomes. This gap is notable given strong evidence that NREM slow-wave activity is critical for hippocampal-dependent memory consolidation and that chronic sleep disruption in youth is linked to adverse brain and cognitive development.

**Study Design and Data Description:**

This longitudinal study leverages remote mobile health (mHealth) assessments and home EEG monitoring to characterize how AT alters sleeping brain rhythms and how these changes relate to cognition and mental health. Adolescents with OSA and matched healthy controls will be assessed at three timepoints (baseline \~2 weeks pre-surgery; 3 and 6 months post-surgery). Sleep will be measured objectively using home EEG headbands to quantify sleep duration, architecture, efficiency, fragmentation, and NREM spectral power, complemented by daily sleep diaries. Cognition (declarative memory, working memory, executive function), depressive symptoms, and sleep patterns will be assessed via mHealth apps, including a sleep-dependent Minecraft-based spatial memory task.   
    
**Objective**

The objective is to characterize day-to-day and longitudinal associations among sleeping brain rhythms, cognition, and depressive symptoms of the participants. Although data was collected pre and post surgery, your task will be to focus on the day-to-day changes, impact of sleep nightly sleep variability in time, architecture, and quality, on cognitive performance before surgery. We hypothesize that children with OSA will perform worse on cognitive tasks compared to age/sex matched controls.
